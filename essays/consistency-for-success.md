I can’t count the number of times I’ve been to a restaurant, where I discover a delicious five-star dish, but absolutely disappointed another day when I order the same exact menu item. The same can be said about service; you get a lovely and pleasant waiter one day but a rude waiter another day. Food quality and service are no doubt the driving factors of a successful restaurant, but the essential element that determines how successful a business is, is consistency. There’s no wonder, finding a good place to eat cannot be solely decided by Yelp reviews. The importance of consistency translates to all types of businesses, systems, and anything with a structure, especially when it comes to coding as a software engineer. 

Coding standards are necessary in software engineering because it increases efficiency and minimizes complexity, particularly when collaboration is involved. Having a well-defined environment in code is crucial because the result is easy readability and easy maintenance for collaborators. Not only does it benefit others when code is uniform, but it also results in efficient workflow for yourself. Spacing, indenting, syntax, and other conventions make a world of a difference when reviewing and debugging code. If you can’t effectively understand your own code, how is anyone else going to have an easier time? A specific recipe is pointless if the exact measurements and directions aren’t followed in the same way by all chefs and cooks in the kitchen. 

After my first week of experiencing ESLint with IntelliJ, I realize that having a well-defined structure and standard for coding has helped me improve my learning and efficiency of coding in JavaScript. At first, it was a bit frustrating to go back through my code and fix issues that didn’t comply with ESLint standards. However, with more practice, I acquired a better habit of writing code that resulted in fewer and fewer errors. One thing I haven’t gotten used to yet is ESLint throwing a bunch of red squiggly lines for errors such as ‘defined but never used’ variables and functions, until it is actually used. Overall, my experience with ESLint thus far has benefited me in becoming a more efficient programmer.
