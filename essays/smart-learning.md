Effective questioning benefits the learning experience of the person asking and the relevant community. It is essential for software engineers to develop a good habit of asking smart questions because it shapes the way you think and how you organize your thoughts. By dissecting a problem and identifying what you do understand and what you don’t, you are effectively recalling and applying learned information, while also isolating specific problems you are trying to comprehend. This is especially beneficial for software engineers in debugging situations to break down problems and learning where to focus your efforts to avoid wasting time. In acknowledging information that you understand and describing specific approaches to the problem results in more effective and insightful answers, and may provide knowledge for others as well. Thought provoking questions lead to more profound conversations and deeper understanding of a particular subject.

<https://stackoverflow.com/questions/43871637/no-access-control-allow-origin-header-is-present-on-the-requested-resource-whe/>
In the subject header for this post, it states the object of the problem and the deviated behavior that resulted, which is "No 'Access-Control-Allow-Origin' header is present on the requested resource -- when trying to get data from a REST API." This displays a clear and specific problem, so that looking at the header alone, others can determine whether it can be a question they can answer or not. The body of the post establishes their goal, provides snippets of code and error messages, provides some understanding of why there is an error, and shows that they put effort into solving the problem themselves first. The resulting answer that was accepted as the best was extremely detailed  and straightforward. Not only did it benefit the person who asked the question, but it most likely provided some helpful knowledge for some of the 1.3 million viewers of the community.

<https://stackoverflow.com/questions/63834381/is-it-possible-to-mock-a-method-that-is-both-static-and-void-using-mockito/>
The subject header of this Stack Overflow question asks, “Is it possible to mock a method that is both static and void using Mockito?” To summarize the body of the post, he/she is looking for an alternate approach to a problem to avoid using a mentioned method, and very broadly asks, “Is there a way to do it or is refactoring legacy code the only way?” along with several snippets of code pertaining to their problem. Firstly, the question is closed and deserves a simple ‘yes’ or ‘no’ answer, and the subject header is not in object-deviation form. There is no provided proof that any prior research was done, and no details about what it is they are looking to accomplish. The first answer starts off with another question asking, “What do you want to happen when the mocked method gets called?” thus confirming that not enough information was provided to effectively answer the question.   

Although the chosen question for the "smart way" is comparatively superior to the latter, it isn't perfect. Through this experience of searching for smart questions on Stack Overflow, I realized that it's difficult to come across smart questions and extremely easy to spot ones that lacked effort. Learning about how to effectivly ask questions opened new doors in my learning experience and I am determined to apply this way of thinking and questioning.
