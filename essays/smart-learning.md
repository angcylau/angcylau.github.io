Effective questioning benefits the learning experience of the person asking and the relevant community. It is essential for software engineers to develop a good habit of asking smart questions because it shapes the way you think and how you organize your thoughts. By dissecting a problem and identifying what you do understand and what you don’t, you are effectively recalling and applying learned information, while also isolating specific problems you are trying to comprehend. This is especially beneficial for software engineers in debugging situations to break down problems and learning where to focus your efforts to avoid wasting time. In acknowledging information that you understand and describing specific approaches to the problem results in more effective and insightful answers, and may provide knowledge for others as well.

The subject header of the question asks, “Is it possible to mock a method that is both static and void using Mockito?” To summarize the body of the post, he/she is looking for an alternate approach to a problem to avoid using a mentioned method, and very broadly asks, “Is there a way to do it or is refactoring legacy code the only way?” along with several snippets of code pertaining to their problem. Firstly, the question is closed and deserves a simple ‘yes’ or ‘no’ answer, and the subject header is not in object-deviation form. There is no provided proof that any prior research was done, and no details about what it is they are looking to accomplish. The first answer starts off with another question asking, “What do you want to happen when the mocked method gets called?” thus confirming that not enough information was provided to effectively answer the question.
